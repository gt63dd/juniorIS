\section{Introduction}
Limited-release online stores create a buying environment where timing matters a lot. Many users try to purchase the same item at almost the exact same moment, and in that kind of setting even a small speed advantage can determine who gets through checkout first. Bots exploit this by moving through storefront flows more quickly and more consistently than most human shoppers can. While real shoppers can also act fast during a high-demand drop, their sessions usually show more variation, more hesitation, and more signs of ordinary browsing before they attempt to complete a purchase.

This is not only a technical problem. It is also a fairness problem. When the same kinds of automated sessions repeatedly win limited releases, legitimate buyers begin to feel that the system is unfair. Over time that damages trust in the storefront and in the release process itself. For large companies, this problem is often addressed with enterprise anti-bot systems that combine device signals, proprietary telemetry, behavioral data, and constantly updated detection rules \cite{10.1145/3447815}. Smaller sellers, however, usually do not have access to that kind of infrastructure. Even when stronger defenses are available, aggressive anti-bot controls can create a different problem by adding unnecessary friction for legitimate customers \cite{10.1145/3672608.3707741}.

This thesis examines whether a lighter and more explainable approach can still be useful. I present DropGuard Lite, a server-side prototype for bot risk scoring that relies on request logs and reconstructed session behavior rather than heavy client-side instrumentation or black-box detection models. The core idea is simple: instead of judging isolated requests one at a time, the system reconstructs a user session from backend activity, extracts behavioral features from that session, and then assigns a risk score based on patterns that appear suspicious in high-demand checkout settings.

The main research question of this thesis is whether ordinary backend data can support a practical and interpretable method for identifying suspicious fast-path checkout behavior. More specifically, I ask whether sessions that are likely bot-driven can be separated from legitimate human sessions using timing, navigation, and action-based patterns derived from server-observable events.

This project is intentionally modest in scope. I do not claim that one policy, one score, or one feature set can solve the broader web bot problem. I also do not claim that a lightweight scoring system can fully replace stronger multi-layer anti-abuse systems. Instead, I focus on a narrower and more practical question: can a smaller storefront use the data it already collects to make better judgments about which sessions deserve closer attention during a competitive release?

The contribution of this thesis is both practical and methodological. On the practical side, DropGuard Lite offers a clear workflow for ingesting storefront activity, reconstructing sessions, extracting interpretable features, and reviewing suspicious traces. On the methodological side, the thesis argues that session-level behavior matters more than isolated requests when studying fast-path checkout automation. A single cart request or checkout request does not say much by itself. What matters is how quickly it appears, what kind of browsing led up to it, how often nearby actions repeat, and how compressed the overall flow looks compared with ordinary human sessions.

The paper proceeds as follows. Section 2 reviews related work on behavioral bot detection, web log analysis, challenge-based defenses, and heavier commercial approaches. Section 3 provides background on why session reconstruction matters in limited-release commerce. Section 4 explains the methodology and the design of the feature set. Section 5 describes the architecture and implementation of the prototype. Section 6 outlines the evaluation design. Section 7 presents the findings from the current system and explains what they suggest. Section 8 discusses the implications, limitations, and meaning of the results. Section 9 concludes with threats to validity and future work.

\section{Related Work}
Prior work on web bot detection suggests that there is no single universal signal that cleanly separates automated traffic from legitimate user activity. Instead, different strands of research highlight different sources of evidence, each with its own tradeoffs. For this project, four themes are especially important: behavioral detection, server-log analysis, challenge-based defenses, and richer multi-signal detection systems.

One important theme is behavioral bot detection. Research in this area suggests that the way actions unfold over time can reveal more than simple counts of requests or static header information. Timing regularity, short transition windows, and repeated action patterns can all help indicate suspicious activity. Rahman and Tomar, for example, argue that behavior-oriented features can strengthen detection by focusing on how requests are generated rather than only on what resources are requested \cite{rahman2020new}. Tanaka and colleagues similarly show that combining user behavior with other request-level information can improve log-based bot detection \cite{tanaka2020bot}. The main takeaway from this line of work is that suspicious traffic often becomes easier to identify when viewed as a sequence rather than as a collection of independent requests.

A second theme is web log analysis and forensic investigation. This literature matters a great deal for my project because DropGuard Lite is built on the assumption that ordinary backend logs contain meaningful evidence. Stassopoulou and Dikaiakos showed early on that web robot detection could be framed probabilistically using server-side traces \cite{stassopoulou2009web}. Later work by Rahman and Tomar extended that idea by showing that request logs can support bot-oriented forensic analysis even without relying heavily on rich client instrumentation \cite{rahman2020forensic}. This is important because many smaller systems already collect backend logs for monitoring and debugging, which means session-level analysis can potentially be added without requiring a complete redesign of the storefront.

A third area includes challenge-based and rule-based defenses such as CAPTCHAs, queues, thresholds, and other friction mechanisms. These approaches remain common because they are relatively understandable and can block certain forms of abuse. However, they also create usability costs. A CAPTCHA or queue may successfully slow down bots, but it can also frustrate legitimate customers, especially during a time-sensitive release. Recent work such as Swiss Cheese CAPTCHA shows that challenge design continues to evolve, but it also reinforces the larger point that challenge systems involve a tradeoff between protection and user experience \cite{10.1145/3672608.3707741}. This tradeoff is one reason a risk-based approach is appealing. If suspicious sessions can be scored first, stronger friction can be applied more selectively.

A fourth theme is the rise of heavier anti-bot systems that combine multiple streams of evidence, such as device fingerprints, mouse dynamics, browser metadata, and broader telemetry. Iliou and coauthors, for instance, show that behavioral biometrics and log signals together can strengthen advanced web bot detection \cite{10.1145/3447815}. At the same time, later work on bot evasion using deep reinforcement learning makes clear that attackers can adapt to detection strategies over time \cite{10.1145/3538969.3538994}. This matters for my thesis in two ways. First, it shows why simple static rules are rarely enough on their own. Second, it highlights the practical gap between what large commercial systems can do and what smaller storefronts can realistically deploy.

The gap my thesis focuses on is therefore not whether richer systems can outperform simpler ones. They often can. The more relevant question is whether a smaller and more transparent system can still provide meaningful value in practice. Many storefronts need a baseline they can actually understand, inspect, and adjust. A lightweight system that reconstructs sessions, computes interpretable features, and supports human review may be less powerful than enterprise anti-bot infrastructure, but it may still be much more deployable.

My work differs from prior literature in emphasis rather than in claiming a completely new detection principle. The novelty here is the combination of scope, design choice, and deployment target. DropGuard Lite focuses specifically on fast-path checkout behavior in limited-release storefronts. It relies primarily on backend activity rather than heavy telemetry. It treats explainability as a core design requirement rather than an afterthought. And instead of presenting detection as an all-or-nothing classification problem, it frames the output as a session-level risk score that can support different levels of intervention.

In that sense, this project is best understood as a practical middle ground. It sits between naive threshold rules and large commercial anti-bot platforms. The goal is not to compete with the most sophisticated systems. The goal is to show that a smaller system can still capture useful evidence, especially when operators need something they can audit, reason about, and improve over time.

\section{Background}
Drop-based online commerce is built around scarcity and timing. Products are often released in small quantities, and many users try to buy them as soon as they become available. This environment creates a strong incentive for automation. Fast-path bot behavior in these settings often appears as short sessions that move quickly into cart and checkout, repeat certain actions several times, and show very little normal browsing before purchase attempts begin.

Because of that, individual requests usually do not tell the full story. What matters more is the order and timing of actions across an entire session. Session reconstruction makes it possible to group related requests together and view them as a sequence rather than as isolated events. Once that sequence is visible, patterns such as burstiness, shallow navigation, repeated retries, and compressed transitions become easier to identify \cite{rahman2020forensic,stassopoulou2009web}.

This thesis focuses on signals that can be observed from the server side. That choice is deliberate. It leaves out heavier approaches such as advanced fingerprinting or detailed behavioral biometrics, which may improve detection but also add more integration work and more governance concerns \cite{10.1145/3447815}. It also avoids treating challenge-based defenses as the main source of evidence, since tools like CAPTCHAs and queues can add friction for real users \cite{10.1145/3672608.3707741}. The question here is whether ordinary request data can still provide useful information for bot detection.

This scope also makes the system easier to think about from a deployment standpoint. Most web applications already collect request logs for debugging, monitoring, or analytics. Using that existing data lowers the barrier to implementation and makes the results easier to audit. When a session is flagged, reviewers can trace that outcome back to concrete request behavior instead of relying on a model that is difficult to explain. That matters in high-demand release settings, where users may challenge outcomes and operators need a clear reason for why a session was flagged or allowed.

\section{Methodology}
The aim of this methodology is to identify suspicious fast-path sessions using backend request data and reconstructed session histories. The approach assumes access to ordinary request information such as timestamps, request paths, HTTP methods, response codes, and some usable session key or fallback grouping method. It does not depend on detailed device fingerprinting or outside threat intelligence.

The first step is session reconstruction. Requests are grouped into ordered sequences so that behavior can be interpreted in context. This step is necessary because the meaning of any single request depends on what comes before and after it. A cart request, for example, may be completely normal in one session and suspicious in another depending on how quickly it appears and what kind of browsing led up to it \cite{rahman2020forensic,stassopoulou2009web}.

Once sessions are reconstructed, the next step is feature extraction. The features used in this project focus on three main areas: timing, navigation, and action patterns. Timing-related features include burstiness, consistency between requests, and overall session duration. Navigation-related features include how many parts of the site a session visits, how deep the browsing goes, and how quickly it moves into purchase-critical endpoints. Action-based features look at the balance between normal browsing and purchase-oriented actions, as well as the concentration of retries near important parts of the checkout flow. Where metadata is available, basic consistency checks can also be included.

These features are then combined into a session-level risk score. In the current version of the system, the scoring logic is policy-based rather than black-box. That is important because it allows reviewers to understand why a session received a particular score and makes it easier to tune thresholds based on context. Depending on the score, the system can support different kinds of responses, such as passive monitoring, manual review, queue placement, temporary throttling, or additional verification.

The scope of this approach is intentionally limited. It is designed for fast-path bot behavior in environments where checkout activity can be observed through logs. It is not meant to be a complete answer to all forms of automation. More advanced bots that closely imitate human pacing or spread activity across many identities may be harder to catch with this method alone \cite{10.1145/3538969.3538994}.

The design of the methodology also reflects a practical concern. The features are chosen not only because they might help distinguish suspicious sessions, but also because they can be extracted reliably and explained clearly. If a feature is too noisy or too hard to interpret, it becomes less useful in a system like this. Keeping the feature set readable also makes it easier to revise the scoring logic as more evidence comes in.

Another important part of the design is the separation between evidence and policy. The features describe what happened in a session, while the thresholds determine how aggressively the system responds. That separation matters because different storefronts may have different tolerance for false positives. One site may prefer conservative intervention, while another may accept more aggressive screening during especially competitive drops. By keeping thresholds adjustable, the system allows those tradeoffs to be made openly instead of burying them inside the feature pipeline.

\section{System Design and Implementation}
DropGuard Lite is implemented as a prototype built with FastAPI, SQLite, and a simple React interface for reviewing storefront and session data. The goal of the system is to provide a straightforward path from raw activity logs to session-level analysis that can support bot risk scoring.

The system takes in storefront activity related to products, cart actions, and checkout behavior. Telemetry is captured through HTTP middleware along with selected client events. Each event record includes information such as a session ID, event type, timestamp, endpoint context, and optional metadata.

From there, the processing pipeline moves through five main stages. First, the system ingests incoming events. Second, it updates or inserts session summaries in storage. Third, it reconstructs ordered session timelines from the recorded events. Fourth, it computes behavioral features for each session. Finally, it exposes analysis outputs that can be reviewed directly or used for downstream scoring decisions.

The outputs are centered on the session level. These include ordered event histories, feature summaries, and identifiers that can later support risk decisions. This structure is useful both for evaluation and for explainability. Instead of seeing only a final label, a reviewer can inspect the actual sequence of events and understand which parts of the session contributed to concern.

At the current stage of the project, telemetry capture, session reconstruction, and feature extraction are already working. The broader score-policy layer and full mitigation flow are still being developed. Making that distinction clear is important because this is still a draft-stage system, not a finished production tool.

The architecture is also intentionally modular. The API layer handles storefront requests and telemetry intake. The storage layer keeps session and event records. The analytics layer generates features and summary views. The front end supports session inspection and review. Keeping these responsibilities separate makes the prototype easier to test and extend. It also makes adoption more flexible, since a team could begin with passive monitoring and only later add automated responses.

Session lifecycle management is another part of the implementation. Sessions are time-bounded, consent status is tracked where applicable, and older telemetry is removed based on retention rules. These details matter because they affect both data quality and the way the system would behave in practice. They also help keep the project aligned with concerns around data minimization, reviewability, and reasonable storage limits.

\section{Evaluation Setup}
The evaluation is designed to test whether session-level behavior derived from logs can help separate suspicious fast-path activity from legitimate human checkout behavior during high-demand releases.

The setup uses two broad comparison groups. The first is legitimate human sessions collected in the project environment. The second is suspicious or bot-like sessions represented through controlled automated traces or rule-labeled patterns. Because the quality of those labels directly affects the strength of the analysis, label quality is treated as an important part of validity rather than as a background assumption.

The evaluation focuses on three main criteria. The first is separability, meaning whether suspicious sessions show recognizable behavioral patterns that differ from legitimate ones. The second is interpretability, meaning whether higher-risk sessions can be explained in terms of visible evidence from the trace. The third is practical usefulness, meaning whether the output of the system could support realistic decisions in a storefront setting without requiring a heavy infrastructure stack.

The final version of the evaluation will report standard measures such as precision, recall, false positives, and confusion patterns, along with ranked review of high-risk sessions. That ranked view matters because, in practice, operators often focus on the sessions that look most suspicious first rather than treating every case the same way.

Since the full experiments are still underway, this section describes the evaluation design rather than claiming final numerical results. That is intentional. The purpose at this stage is to show how the thesis will test the central question in a way that is both practical and explainable.

To make the evaluation more useful, the project also includes case-level walkthroughs of representative sessions across different risk bands. These walkthroughs are meant to show not only whether a session appears suspicious, but also why. That kind of review helps reveal weak logic early and keeps the scoring system tied to understandable evidence instead of vague patterns.

The setup also includes sensitivity checks around thresholds and event windows. Drop behavior can shift quickly, especially around release time, so a feature or threshold that looks reasonable in one window may not behave the same way in another. Testing that sensitivity helps show whether the system is stable enough to be useful and whether certain settings need to adapt over time.

\section{Results and Findings}
Because the full evaluation is still in progress, this section focuses on qualitative findings rather than final metrics. Even so, the current traces already suggest several patterns that appear repeatedly in sessions that look more suspicious.

One pattern is speed through purchase-critical parts of the site. Sessions that appear more suspicious often move into cart and checkout with very little buildup. Compared with typical human sessions, they tend to show less browsing depth and less variation before purchase-oriented actions begin.

Timing also appears to matter. Candidate suspicious sessions often show tighter spacing between requests, especially around release windows. In some cases, the timing looks more regular than what would usually be expected from a human shopper moving through the site manually. Bursts of repeated actions near cart and checkout endpoints also show up frequently in bot-like traces.

At the same time, the project also makes the ambiguity very clear. Highly motivated human users can behave aggressively during a competitive drop. Some users may move quickly, refresh often, or reach checkout with very little browsing. Certain mobile or cached flows may also look compressed in ways that resemble automation. That overlap is one reason the system should not rely on a single signal or a single threshold.

The final version of this section will add quantitative comparisons between session groups, confusion analysis, and a clearer picture of which signals contribute most strongly to high-risk scores. For now, one useful result is that the pipeline already produces session summaries that can be inspected directly. That makes it possible to compare traces, test assumptions, and see where the current evidence is strong or weak before the final metrics are finished.

Another early takeaway is that the system works better when evidence is combined. No single feature appears reliable enough on its own during high-demand drop events. Timing helps, but timing alone is not enough. Navigation depth helps, but it can also be misleading in some cases. Retry behavior matters, but even that needs context. The more useful approach seems to be combining these signals so the score reflects a fuller picture of the session.

\section{Analysis and Discussion}
The main point that comes out of the current analysis is that server-side behavioral signals can still be useful, even without a heavy detection stack. Looking at behavior at the session level matters a lot here. Suspicious activity is usually easier to spot in the overall sequence of actions than in a single request by itself.

This project supports a middle-ground approach to bot detection. Simpler methods are easier to deploy, easier to inspect, and easier to adjust. At the same time, they are also likely to be less powerful than larger systems that combine many types of telemetry. Even with that limitation, a simpler system can still be worthwhile, especially for smaller storefronts that need something understandable and manageable.

The limits of the approach also need to be stated clearly. Attackers can adapt over time and change the way their sessions look \cite{10.1145/3538969.3538994}. Legitimate users in high-pressure release windows may also resemble bots on basic timing features. Because of that, static one-feature rules are not enough. A more careful combination of signals is necessary, along with periodic review of how the thresholds are behaving.

Compared with using challenge tools everywhere, a risk-based approach can apply friction more selectively and may reduce the number of legitimate users who are affected unnecessarily \cite{10.1145/3672608.3707741}. That is one of the main reasons this kind of system is appealing. It gives operators a way to reserve stronger interventions for the sessions that raise the most concern.

The project also suggests a practical path for deployment. A storefront could begin by using the system only for monitoring and review. From there, the team could study false positives, refine the features, and only then connect the scores to stronger interventions. That step-by-step approach lowers the risk of adopting the system too aggressively and makes policy changes easier to explain.

Another important point is that interpretability should remain important even if the system becomes more sophisticated later. If richer data or learned models are added, the system should still preserve clear reasons for why a session is considered risky. Otherwise, it becomes harder to audit the system and harder to justify its decisions.

\section{Conclusion}
This thesis examines whether session-level scoring based on server logs can help identify suspicious fast-path checkout automation in limited-release online commerce. DropGuard Lite contributes a clear workflow for reconstructing sessions, extracting behavior-based features, and using those features to support risk-informed decisions.

The contribution of the project is practical. It offers a method that smaller storefronts could realistically begin with, even if they do not have access to a large commercial anti-bot platform. The project treats detection as a problem of session behavior rather than isolated requests, and it keeps the scoring logic separate from the response policy so that interventions can be tuned based on context.

There are still important threats to validity. Labels may be imperfect, aggressive human behavior may overlap with suspicious behavior, and results may depend on the particular storefront or release pattern being studied. The quality and detail of the logs also place limits on what the system can infer.

The current prototype has clear limitations as well. It relies on server-observable behavior, may miss more advanced bots that imitate human patterns closely, and is not presented as a complete solution to bot abuse. The final quantitative evaluation is also still in progress.

Future work could strengthen the project in several ways. One direction is broader validation across different storefronts and release settings. Another is the use of more sequence-aware modeling. A third is combining interpretable rules with learned models while still preserving transparency. Thresholds could also be adapted based on the phase of a drop, and the scoring system could be integrated more tightly with queueing or mitigation workflows.

Overall, DropGuard Lite shows that bot-risk scoring built from existing backend data is worth studying as a realistic starting point. It does not replace a full layered anti-abuse program, but it can still provide useful evidence in environments where operators need a system that is understandable, reproducible, and easier to deploy. More broadly, the project offers a concrete path from a research question to a workflow that could be improved over time as stronger evidence and better calibration become available.
